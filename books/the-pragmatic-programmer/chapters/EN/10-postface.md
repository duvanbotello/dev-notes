# 10 - Postface

> Quick navigation: [Core idea](#core-idea) · [Key concepts](#key-concepts) · [Actions](#actions) · [Related notes](#related-notes)

## Core idea

The postface closes the book with an ethical and personal message: developing software means actively shaping the future. That privilege carries power, and that power demands direct responsibility for the consequences of what we ship to users.

## Key concepts

- **Life is shaped by decisions:** in the long run, we shape our lives and ourselves; the process ends only when we die.
- **Non-transferable responsibility:** the decisions we make as developers are, ultimately, our own responsibility.
- **Privilege of building the future:** as developers, we have extraordinary capacity for impact.
- **Power and responsibility are linked:** the more power our technical work has, the greater our ethical duty.
- **User-protection question:** before shipping code, we should ask: "Have I protected the user?"
- **Personal-integrity question:** we should also ask: "Would I use this myself?"
- **Nobody is perfect:** everyone overlooks things from time to time; perfection is not realistic.
- **Duty of sincere diligence:** even if we cannot predict everything, we must try to list consequences and protect users from them.
- **Responsibility when things fail:** if we cannot honestly say we made that effort, we share responsibility when things go wrong.

## Quotes (short only)

- "In the long run, we shape our lives and we shape ourselves." - Quote mentioned in the postface
- "Have I protected the user?" - Guiding question from the postface
- "Would I use this?" - Guiding question from the postface

## My interpretation

This ending reminds me that writing code is not just task execution: it is decision-making that affects real people. The strongest part for me is the standard of professional honesty: we may not prevent every error, but we are still obligated to act with diligence, think through consequences, and protect users before shipping.

## Practical lessons

- Add an ethical review step to every delivery, together with technical review.
- Evaluate each change with two explicit questions: "Have I protected the user?" and "Would I use this myself?"
- Identify foreseeable security, privacy, and misuse risks before deployment.
- Document sensitive decisions and trade-offs to keep responsibility traceable.
- Own mistakes transparently when they happen and reinforce safeguards to avoid repetition.

## Questions

- What foreseeable negative outcomes could this change create for users?
- Are we protecting users by design, or only reacting after incidents?
- What evidence supports that I would personally use what I am about to release?
- Which decisions in this cycle need stronger traceability because of their impact?
- If something fails, could we honestly defend that we did everything reasonable to prevent it?

## Actions

- [ ] Add a small ethical checklist to the Definition of Done with the two postface questions.
- [ ] For high-impact PRs, document user risks and mitigations before approval.
- [ ] Include abuse scenarios and foreseeable failure reviews in critical changes.
- [ ] Record sensitive technical decisions with owners, context, and rationale.
- [ ] In retrospectives, review recent incidents and identify where preventive diligence was missing.
