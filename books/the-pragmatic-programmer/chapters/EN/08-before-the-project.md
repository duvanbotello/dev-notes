# 08 - Before the project

> Quick navigation: [Core idea](#core-idea) · [Key concepts](#key-concepts) · [Actions](#actions) · [Related notes](#related-notes)

## Core idea

Before building, we need to discover. This chapter argues that requirements are not a fixed checklist, but an ongoing conversation where the team helps the client understand what they really need, uses frequent feedback to adjust direction, and keeps design ready for change without losing technical clarity.

## Key concepts

- **The requirements pit:** nobody knows exactly what they want at the start; discovery is part of the job.
- **Programming as therapy:** developers help clarify needs by exposing implications and alternatives.
- **Requirements exploration:** interpret what the client says and contrast it with real consequences.
- **Creative and intellectual process:** defining requirements demands fast thinking, judgment, and collaboration.
- **Feedback as the central mechanism:** generate feedback and use it to improve decisions.
- **Short iterations with the client:** small cycles reduce interpretation errors and accelerate learning.
- **Requirements vs. policy:** policy should be treated as configurable metadata, not hard-coded rules.
- **Working code as evidence:** real requirements validation happens in software that runs, not in long documents.
- **Avoid overspecification:** requirements describe needs; they should not become architecture, design, or UI.
- **Shared glossary:** common vocabulary prevents misunderstandings across business and engineering.
- **Cognitive unblocking:** when stuck, switching context helps the brain reorganize ideas.
- **Explain the problem to someone else:** conversation unlocks hypotheses and improves solutions.
- **Luck and preparation:** intuition improves when there is enough prior knowledge and experience.
- **Truly working together:** collaboration means debating and building, not only collecting notes.
- **Critique code, not people:** ego-free collaboration improves quality and team learning.
- **Agility as response capacity:** being agile means reacting to change and uncertainty, not adopting a label.
- **Good design enables change:** solid design lowers friction when new requirements appear.

## My interpretation

The strongest idea is that requirements work is not request capture, but facilitated discovery. If we treat requirements as static, we end up defending documents; if we treat them as hypotheses, we can iterate with evidence and build better solutions. It is also key that agility depends as much on technical design as on the quality of conversations with the client and the team.

## Practical lessons

- Turn every requirement into a conversation about implications, limits, and trade-offs.
- Show small functional increments to get early client feedback.
- Separate stable business rules from changing policies to adapt with less friction.
- Maintain and review a project glossary for critical terms.
- Use deliberate breaks and technical conversations to resolve complex blocks.
- Practice active collaboration (pairing or group work) with focus on improving code, not winning arguments.

## Questions

- Which current requirements are still unvalidated assumptions?
- Which policies are hard-coded and should become configurable?
- How often does our delivery cycle receive real client feedback?
- Does the team share the same meaning for key domain terms?
- Does our current design enable requirement changes or make them expensive?

## Actions

- [ ] Define one short iteration with a functional demo and direct client feedback.
- [ ] Create or update a project glossary with business and technical terms.
- [ ] Review one ambiguous requirement and document its implications with concrete examples.
- [ ] Identify one hard-coded policy and move it to editable configuration.
- [ ] Resolve one technical block by explaining the problem out loud with another team member.
- [ ] In a pairing session, review code using the rule: critique code, not people.
